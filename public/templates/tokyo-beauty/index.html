Ritual · 04

How a residence comes into being

01

Inhabitation Study

We spend 72 hours on the site across three seasons before drawing a single line. We record wind direction, shadow movement, soil resonance, and the specific quality of silence at 4am. The land speaks first.

02

Negative Drafting

We design the voids before the walls. Each room begins as an absence — a specific volume of stillness — then we determine the minimum material needed to hold that absence in place. Nothing is added. Things are removed.

03

Material Dialogue

Fourteen months of construction with a fixed team of nine artisans. Hinoki cypress aged seven years. Concrete poured at 4°C for density. Every joint is hand-finished. The building does not perform — it simply is.

Approach · 06

What distinguishes a Kurogane residence

+

Seasonal Calibration

Every room is tuned to perform differently across four seasons. The study faces east in winter for morning warmth. The bedroom captures the specific frequency of autumn rain on aged copper.

+

Acoustic Architecture

Sound is treated as a material. Wall density, ceiling height, and courtyard geometry are calculated to produce a specific silence — not the absence of sound, but the presence of stillness.

+

One-Generation Commitment

We design for the person who commissions the work. Not for resale value. Not for posterity. The house fits one life exactly, the way a garment fits one body.

Oracle · 09

Questions we are asked

You write to us. Not a brief — a letter. Tell us about the land, or about the way you live, or about what you are trying to stop doing. We read every letter. If there is alignment, we visit the site within sixty days. There is no fee for the first conversation.
Our commissions begin at ¥180,000,000 and have reached ¥600,000,000. The variance is not about size — it is about material complexity, site difficulty, and the depth of seasonal calibration required. We discuss this openly after the first site visit.
Rarely. We have completed two residences outside the Kansai region — one in Karuizawa, one in Naoshima. The land must justify the distance. We do not accept international commissions.
Because the inhabitation study alone requires 72 hours across three seasons. Because our team of nine artisans works on one project at a time. Because architecture built in haste remembers its haste. We prefer that our buildings forget how they were made.
Two of our seventeen residences are available for private viewing by appointment. We do not publish addresses. If you are considering a commission, we arrange a visit as part of the initial conversation.
Most architects begin with structure and fill it with space. We begin with the space — the specific volume of stillness a room requires — and then determine what material is necessary to hold it. The walls serve the void, not the other way around. This is not philosophy. It is our literal drafting method.
